Question 1: Correlation vs. Causation

The map created by John Snow showed that many deaths from Cholera were clustered near the Broad Street pump in London. However, clustering alone only demonstrated correlation, not causation.

To move from correlation to causation, Snow took several important steps:

He interviewed local residents to learn where they obtained their drinking water.
He identified cases of people who lived far away but still became sick after drinking water from the Broad Street pump.
He noticed that workers at a nearby brewery did not get sick because they drank beer instead of pump water.
He compared households using different water sources and found higher infection rates among those using the Broad Street pump.
Finally, he convinced authorities to remove the pump handle, after which the number of new cholera cases declined significantly.

These steps provided strong evidence that contaminated water from the pump was the cause of the outbreak, not just associated with it. 

Question 2: The Danger of Missing Data

If Snow had only collected data from local hospitals, his dataset would have been incomplete and biased.

Hospital records would likely:

include only severe cases
miss people treated at home
exclude deaths that were never officially recorded
ignore important location-based details about where people lived and got their water

By walking through neighborhoods and collecting information directly from households, Snow gathered more accurate and representative data.

Without this field investigation:

the death clusters might not have been clearly identified
the connection to the Broad Street pump could have been weaker
his conclusion about the contaminated water source might not have been convincing

This shows how missing data can lead to incorrect conclusions in data analysis. 📉

Question 3: The “Miasma” of Today

At the time, many people believed in the miasma theory, which claimed diseases spread through bad air. It seemed logical because areas with bad smells also had many deaths, but it was incorrect.

A modern example of a similar assumption could be:

“More working hours always increase productivity.”

Many organizations still assume that longer working hours lead to better performance. However, data analysis often shows that:

productivity decreases after long working hours
employee burnout increases
mistakes become more frequent

Careful data studies suggest that balanced working hours can produce better results than simply working longer.

This example shows how widely accepted beliefs can appear correct on the surface but may change when tested using proper data analysis methods.
